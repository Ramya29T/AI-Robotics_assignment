History of VR and Telerobotics

The intriguing history of VR and Telerobotics

Evolution of Telerobotics

  • 1940s-1950s: One of the earliest instances of technology resembling telerobotics was developed during World War II with remotely controlled bombs and other military devices. The real foundational work began in the 1950s and 1960s. Raymond Goertz, working for the Atomic Energy Commission, designed a mechanical manipulator for handling radioactive substances at a distance.
  • 1970s-1980s: During this period, significant advancements were made, particularly in space and underwater exploration. NASA’s use of remotely controlled vehicles for space exploration, such as the Viking 1 and Viking 2 missions to Mars, showcased the early use of robotic arms that were operated remotely from Earth.
  • 1990s-Present:The development of the Internet and improvements in sensor and actuator technologies facilitated rapid growth in telerobotics. This era saw the rise of surgical robots like the da Vinci Surgical System, which allowed surgeons to operate on patients through small incisions using robotically enhanced instruments.Military applications expanded with the development of unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), such as drones, which are used for reconnaissance and active combat without risking human pilots.

History of Virtual Reality

  • 1960s: The concept of VR was popularized by Ivan Sutherland, a pioneer in computer graphics, who created the first VR head-mounted display system, known as “The Sword of Damocles,” in 1968. This rudimentary device provided immersive, albeit primitive, graphical experiences.
  • 1980s-1990s: The 1980s saw the development of VR systems that included more sophisticated sensory equipment, such as gloves and goggles. Jaron Lanier, founder of VPL Research, coined the term “virtual reality” and developed gear like the DataGlove, EyePhone, and AudioSphere. The 1990s witnessed the release of consumer-grade VR systems, such as the Virtual Boy by Nintendo. Although not successful commercially, these systems introduced the concept of virtual environments to a broader audience.
  • 000s-2010s: A Resurgence in VR- Advances in technology and reduction in costs led to a resurgence of interest in VR. In 2012, Oculus Rift rekindled interest in VR with a successful Kickstarter campaign, leading to its eventual acquisition by Facebook in 2014. Modern VR headsets, such as the HTC Vive, Oculus Rift, and Sony PlayStation VR, began providing high-quality virtual experiences, powered by high-speed computing and advanced graphics.

Convergence of Telerobotics and VR

In recent years, the integration of VR and telerobotics has become increasingly prevalent, providing users with more immersive control of remote devices. This combination is particularly effective in fields such as remote surgery, where surgeons use VR interfaces to control robotic instruments, offering high precision and reducing risks during procedures. Other applications include training simulations for dangerous jobs, remote manipulation in hazardous environments, and enhanced control for exploration robots in space and underwater missions. The continued evolution of both VR and telerobotics holds significant promise for creating more integrated, intuitive, and user-friendly remote interaction systems across various disciplines.
